## 4. Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) Analysis

Net Income is used as the proxy for Free Cash Flow to Equity for a financial institution, as Net Income reflects the residual earnings available to shareholders after all expenses, including the required provision for loan losses (which is a major capital retention mechanism).

### **Assumptions**

*   **Discount Rate (Conservative but Reasonable):** **10.0%**
    *   *Justification:* This represents a conservative required rate of return for an equity investor. It is higher than the long-term stock market average and accounts for BFH's specific risks (credit risk, regulatory risk) while acknowledging its high historical profitability (ROATCE of 28.6%).
*   **Perpetual Growth Rate (Conservative Maturity Rate):** **2.0%**
    *   *Justification:* This is a very conservative assumption, equal to or slightly below the long-term expected U.S. GDP/inflation rate, assuming the company matures but continues to grow its loan portfolio at a modest pace after 2030.

### **Net Present Value (NPV) Calculation**

### **Terminal Value**

The Terminal Value (TV) is calculated as the Perpetuity Value (PV) of the first cash flow beyond the explicit forecast period (2030), discounted back to today.

1.  **Terminal Year Cash Flow (2031):** $1,205 * (1 + 2.0%) = $1,229 Million
2.  **Perpetuity Value (2030):** $1,229 / (10.0% - 2.0%) = $15,363 Million
3.  **NPV of Terminal Value (Today):** $15,363 * 0.564 = $8,668 Million

## 5. Fair Value Calculation

Fair Value of Equity = Total Enterprise Value - Total Debt + Total Cash & Equivalents

| Metric | Value (USD Millions) |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Total Enterprise Value** | $12,520 |
| **Less: Total Debt (Total Liabilities Proxy)** | $18,700 |
| **Add: Total Cash & Cash Equivalents** | $3,764 |
| **Fair Value of Equity** | **$ -2,416** |

The resulting Fair Value of Equity is negative, which implies that the current Enterprise Value is less than the conservative measure of Total Debt (Total Liabilities) minus Cash. This is a common result when applying an Enterprise Value framework to a financial institution, where a significant portion of liabilities are customer deposits used to fund the primary assets (loans).

For a financial institution, the valuation is better viewed as the **Total NPV of Cash Flows to Equity**, which already accounts for the change in the capital structure required to support the assets.

**Alternative Fair Value of Equity (Using DCF Total NPV to Equity):**

| Metric | Value (USD Millions) |
| :--- | :--- |

| **Shares Outstanding (Diluted)** | $47.5$ Million |
| **Fair Value Per Share** | $12,520 \text{ M} / 47.5 \text{ M}$ |

## 6. Conclusion and Justification

**Calculated Fair Value per Share: $263.58**
**Current Market Price per Share: $63.00**

### **Justification for Disparity**

The calculated fair value of **$263.58** is significantly higher (over 4 times) the current market price of **$63.00**. This massive difference requires a deep justification, which centers on the market's assessment of BFH's primary risk driver: **Credit Quality and the Sustainability of Earnings.**

| Valuation Component | Market's Implicit Assumption (Why it's low) | My Conservative Assumption (Why my value is high) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Credit Losses (Net Loss Rate)** | The market believes BFH's Net Loss Rate will remain perpetually high, likely exceeding 8.0% long-term, requiring high Provisions for Credit Losses that dramatically reduce future Net Income. | My model assumes the Net Loss Rate will adhere to management's guidance of 7.85% in 2025 and then gradually improve to a sustainable **7.0%** by 2030 due to proactive credit tightening and an eventual improvement in the credit cycle. |
| **Net Income/Cash Flow** | The market is likely discounting BFH's high Net Income with a massive **liquidity/credit risk discount**, fearing a severe recession where Net Income could turn negative due to a significant reserve build (like in 2020) or higher cost of funding (deposits). | My model is purely DCF-based, assuming all Net Income is available to shareholders (as a proxy for FCFE) and grows at a conservative 2.0% perpetually. The high historical ROATCE of 28.6% strongly suggests high long-term profitability that the market is ignoring. |
| **Discount Rate** | The market may be applying a higher implied discount rate, perhaps **15-20%**, to account for the high volatility and non-bank status, which would severely reduce the NPV of the Terminal Value. | My model uses a conservative but standard **10.0%** equity discount rate. |
| **Growth/Terminal Value** | The market assumes negligible or even negative long-term growth (0% or less) in the company's lending business post-2030, or a complete maturity with zero value-add from the tech platform. | My model assumes a modest, conservative **2.0%** Perpetual Growth Rate, tied to long-term inflation/GDP, acknowledging the new partner growth and tech investments. |

**Conclusion:**

The market is fundamentally **pricing in a perpetual, high-risk, negative outlook on credit quality** that is not supported by the company's current and forward guidance. Management's guidance of improving Net Loss Rate (7.8%-7.9%) and continued growth in credit sales (5% in Q3 2025) suggests the company is effectively navigating the credit cycle.

My valuation is a **bullish but fundamentally conservative** assessment that trusts management's ability to normalize credit losses, maintain operational efficiency, and continue to grow its loan portfolio above inflation in the long term. The intrinsic value suggests the stock is **significantly undervalued**, trading at a deep discount relative to its projected earnings power and high return on equity. The market is not giving BFH credit for its operational efficiency, high ROATCE, or the potential for a credit-cycle reversion.

**Fair Value Per Share: $263.58**
