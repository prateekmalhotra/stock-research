### **4. Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) Analysis**

The net income is used as the cash flow for the next year. The DCF model is adjusted for the return on capital (ROIC) generated from the cumulative Net Income.

**ROIC Assumption:**

The company has historically had a negative GAAP net income, but management is projecting a strong path to profitability. Per the rule, a conservative but reasonable positive ROIC must be used.
*   **Conservative ROIC:** I will use a conservative **8.0%** ROIC. This is a reasonable mid-single-digit return for a growing, innovative medical technology company, yet it is conservative enough to account for execution risk and the fact that its historical GAAP profitability is negative.

**Discount and Maturity Rates:**

*   **Discount Rate (Conservative but Reasonable):** I will use a **9.0%** discount rate. This is conservative for a mid-cap, high-growth medical technology company that has significant debt and is still transitioning to sustained profitability.
*   **Maturity Rate (Conservative):** I will use a conservative terminal growth rate of **2.5%**, reflecting a mature medical device company growing slightly above the long-term inflation rate.

### **Justification and Market Assumption Analysis**

The calculated fair value of **\$46.97** is **50.36% higher** than the current market price of **\$31.24**. This significant difference implies that the market is making much more conservative assumptions about Enovis's future profitability and growth than this model.

**Analysis of the Market's Conservative Assumptions:**

1.  **Skepticism on Profitability and Margins:** While management guides for significant annualized margin expansion and positive adjusted EPS, the company is still reporting a GAAP Net Loss. The market may be discounting the credibility of the "adjusted" metrics and is factoring in a much slower, or possibly no, return to sustained GAAP profitability. The market is likely pricing in a higher probability that:
    *   The projected **60 bps annual operating margin expansion** will not materialize due to integration costs (LEMA), persistent inflation, or greater-than-expected competition.
    *   One-time costs like those related to **European Medical Device Regulation (MDR)** compliance and **tariffs** will continue to be a drag on GAAP earnings, effectively making the "Net Income" figure in the model too optimistic.

2.  **Discount on Acquisition-Driven Growth:** A significant part of the growth narrative is the integration of the LEMA acquisition and success in new product launches in the high-growth Recon segment. The market may be assigning a higher risk premium to this integration, doubting the company's ability to achieve the projected cost synergies (LEMA cost savings) and maintain the high single-digit growth in Recon.

3.  **High Debt Load and Interest Costs:** With Total Debt of over \$1.39 billion and a high interest rate environment, the market may be punishing ENOV for its leveraged balance sheet. The model assumes a constant 5.0% interest expense and no further deleveraging, which is conservative. However, the market may be factoring in a higher long-term cost of debt or a necessary equity raise, which would dilute shareholders and justify a lower share price.

**Conclusion:**

The $\$$**46.97** valuation is based on a conservative but successful execution of management's stated strategy: $\sim$6% revenue growth, $\sim$60 bps annual operating margin expansion, and a reasonable long-term ROIC for the sector. The market's current $\$$**31.24** price suggests an underlying belief that Enovis will struggle to achieve sustained profitability, that its growth will slow faster than projected, and/or that the GAAP losses will persist for a longer period. For the fair value to equal the current price, the annual revenue growth from 2026 onwards would need to be closer to **2.5%**, or the projected operating margin expansion would need to be closer to **0 bps**, effectively pricing the company as a low-to-no growth medical device company that never realizes its operating leverage. This model is a case for ENOV being undervalued if management executes successfully on its guided operational improvements.
