## Business Summary

Prairie Operating Co. is an energy exploration and production company that focuses on finding and extracting crude oil and natural gas, primarily operating within the highly productive Permian Basin across West Texas and New Mexico. The company's core business involves leasing land rights, drilling wells, and using specialized technology to bring these raw hydrocarbons to the surface. PROP does not sell fuel directly to consumers; instead, its customers are other large businesses, such as refineries, pipeline operators, and utility companies that process the raw commodities. The company generates nearly all its revenue from the wholesale sale of the extracted oil and gas.

---

## Business Model Risk

The most significant near-term risk for PROP is the extreme volatility of commodity prices. Historically, oil and gas prices have experienced sharp, immediate declines due to global events or sudden changes in supply, which instantly reduces PROP's revenue and ability to fund new drilling operations. A major long-term risk involves the global transition toward cleaner energy. As governments enforce stricter environmental regulations regarding emissions and drilling practices, the cost of operating could rise substantially, potentially challenging their market position over the next decade. Furthermore, the business is constantly threatened by operational risks, such as dry wells (drilling in areas with little or no recoverable oil) or escalating costs for labor and drilling materials, which can quickly erode profits, a common historical concern in the energy extraction industry.

## The History of Prairie Operating Co. (PROP): From Crypto to Crude

### **1. The Inception and Founding Goal (Early 2020s)**

1.  **A Company with a New Identity:** Prairie Operating Co. is a relatively new entity, having been rebranded from its previous life as a cryptocurrency mining company called **Creek Road Miners, Inc.**.
2.  **The Major Business Pivot:** The company officially began its transition away from cryptocurrency and focused entirely on oil and gas production around late 2023, completing the sale of its crypto assets in January 2024.
3.  **The Core Vision for Oil & Gas:** The partners and CEO, Edward Kovalik, *re-launched* the company to fill a perceived gap in the energy market. Their goal was to create a **high-growth oil and gas company** at a time when most U.S. energy producers were focused on slower, steadier growth and paying dividends.
4.  **A Unique Investment Proposition:** They sought to offer investors a rare public market opportunity to invest in a company focused on aggressive growth and compounding capital, which is typically a strategy only seen in private equity firms.

### **2. Current Operations and Core Business**

5.  **Focus Area and Resources:** Today, Prairie is an independent energy company focused on developing and acquiring oil, natural gas, and natural gas liquids (NGLs) in the **Denver-Julesburg (DJ) Basin** of Colorado.
6.  **Targeting Specific Rock Formations:** Their operations are concentrated on two specific rock layers, or "formations," in the basin known as the **Niobrara and Codell**, which are known to be rich in oil and natural gas liquids.
7.  **The Strategy: Buy, Develop, and Grow:** The company's main business model is a "roll-up" story—they find smaller and mid-sized existing oil and gas assets and then acquire them to build a larger production base. They then actively develop these properties by drilling new, efficient wells.

### **3. Headwinds and Challenges Faced**

8.  **The Difficulty of a Major Pivot:** The most significant challenge was the complete **operational transition** from a cryptocurrency company to an exploration and production (E&P) energy company, which requires massive changes in assets, expertise, and operational focus.
9.  **Financial Constraints:** Like many aggressive growth companies, Prairie faced a **compressed timeline to profitability** and was dealing with a relatively **short cash runway**. This risk was clear to investors.
10. **A "Growth Story" in a "Mature Industry":** They operate in the U.S. shale industry, which is generally considered mature and not typically focused on rapid growth, forcing the company to stand out against established norms.
11. **Competition from Giants:** Despite being a small player, they are surrounded by major oil companies like Chevron, Occidental, and Civitas, which own the majority of the adjacent land and have deep financial resources.
12. **Market Volatility and Perception:** The company’s stock has experienced **heightened volatility** and a **"market disconnect,"** meaning the company's strong operational progress was sometimes not reflected in its share price.

### **4. Innovative Solutions and Favorable Tailwinds**

13. **Cost-Efficient Drilling (Solution):** A key success was aggressively **driving down the cost of drilling and completing wells**. They lowered the expense per well significantly, which means they can make a profit even when oil prices are lower.
14. **Leveraging Neighbors' Expertise (Tailwind):** Operating near major, long-established players allows Prairie to hire the same expert vendors and consultants who have already perfected the latest drilling and "fracking" techniques in the area, letting them operate efficiently from day one.
15. **Smart Financial Strategy (Solution/Tailwind):** To fund their growth without excessively selling new shares (dilution), they secured a large, low-cost **Reserve-Based Lending (RBL) credit facility** from a major bank, which is essentially a low-interest loan based on the value of their oil and gas reserves.
16. **Advanced Drilling Techniques (Solution):** They have adopted innovative methods like **"U-turn" and "multi-bench" wells**, which are advanced drilling techniques designed to extract more resources from the ground with greater efficiency.
17. **Sustainable Operations Focus:** They emphasize **capital efficiency** and **environmental responsibility**, even partnering with an electric frack fleet to reduce their environmental footprint, which is a key advantage in a basin that faces environmental scrutiny.

### **5. Present Day and Future Goal**

18. **The Goal: A High-Growth, Mid-Tier Producer:** Prairie’s ultimate goal is to build a high-growth oil and gas company of substantial size, with a focus on maximizing returns and generating consistent cash flow.
19. **Aggressive Growth Trajectory:** The company is currently focused on an aggressive development program—drilling new wells and adding acreage through strategic acquisitions—to deliver significant year-over-year production growth, which is their main measure of success.
20. **Commitment to Responsible Energy:** They position themselves as a company dedicated to providing affordable, reliable energy to meet growing demand while upholding the highest standards of safety and environmental care in their operations.

## Moat Analysis

The moat of Prairie Operating Co. (PROP) is not built on a traditional brand or network effect, but rather on a combination of geographic positioning, engineering expertise, and financial strategy. The company’s rapid growth is driven by an "oil roll-up" strategy focused on acquiring and efficiently developing assets in a specific, high-value region.

### **Analysis of Prairie Operating Co.'s Moat**

1.  **Geographic Moat: High-Quality Core Assets (A Key Differentiator)**
    *   The company concentrates its operations almost entirely in the oil and liquids-rich Denver-Julesburg (DJ) Basin in Colorado, a proven, high-value resource area.
    *   This concentration in a **"core resource play"** is a form of moat because these assets are inherently high-quality and generally have established infrastructure, which lowers the cost and risk of new development compared to frontier exploration.

2.  **Cost and Process Advantage: Mature Field Expertise**
    *   Prairie's primary competitive advantage is its specialization in **revitalizing mature oil fields** and unlocking reserves that competitors may have overlooked or deemed uneconomical.
    *   They achieve this by applying **advanced engineering techniques** and process innovation, such as pioneering "U-turn wells" that target multiple oil-bearing rock layers from a single well, which drives significant cost efficiencies.
    *   This focused, lean operational structure translates to a **low-cost production profile**, allowing the company to remain profitable and competitive even when commodity prices drop.

3.  **Financial Moat: Aggressive Hedging**
    *   The oil and gas industry sells a **commodity product** (oil, natural gas, NGLs), meaning Prairie has little to no special pricing power over market rates.
    *   However, the company mitigates this risk with an **aggressive, long-term hedging program**, which locks in a high percentage of future production at favorable prices, creating a sticky, stable revenue stream for several years and a competitive edge against peers who are more exposed to market volatility.

4.  **Growth Strategy as a Reason for Success (The "Roll-Up")**
    *   The company's significant growth is attributable to a **disciplined roll-up strategy**, where it successfully acquires and integrates over $600 million in producing oil and gas assets to quickly scale its production and revenue.
    *   This approach is likened to a **private equity-stage development case** where management seeks to compound growth rapidly, which is uncommon for a public small-cap energy company and is the reason it grew into what it is today.

### **Displacement, Stickiness, and Innovation**

*   **Ease of Displacement by More Capital:**
    *   A larger competitor with more capital **could displace** Prairie by simply outbidding them on future acquisitions, which is the company's primary growth engine.
    *   However, displacing the *existing production* is difficult, as the resource (oil and gas in the ground) is a permanent asset to which Prairie has legal and operational rights, forming an **asset moat**.
    *   Sustained growth for Prairie relies on successfully financing and integrating its acquisitions; this need for **constant capital** makes it susceptible to large fluctuations in its stock price and high-cost financing, which is a major risk.

*   **Customer/Contracts Stickiness:**
    *   The end product is a commodity, so the sales themselves are generally recurring but not sticky in terms of price; they are sold to refiners and midstream operators at market-based prices.
    *   The "stickiness" in the business model comes from the **long-term financial contracts (hedges)**, which lock in favorable cash flows for years, providing crucial stability that is a moat against commodity-price risk.

*   **R&D and Innovation:**
    *   Prairie Operating Co. does not invest heavily in R&D like a technology company, but it must **constantly innovate in its *operations*** to maintain its edge.
    *   The "innovation" is in **process and engineering excellence**, using next-generation techniques like 3-D seismic mapping and "U-turn wells" to maximize resource recovery and improve capital efficiency in its specific basin.

## Bear Case Scenario

The long thesis for Prairie Operating Co. (PROP) relies heavily on successful execution, a "deeply undervalued" status, and a positive long-term view of a high-growth roll-up strategy. However, a closer look at the company's recent financials and capital structure reveals significant flaws and risks that create a compelling bear case.

### **Critique and Flaws in the Long Thesis**

1.  **Significant Flaw in "Deeply Undervalued" and "Past Issues" Being Resolved:** The stock's perceived undervaluation is contradicted by high valuation metrics like a P/E ratio of 86.73 and an Enterprise Value to EBITDA ratio of 25.32, suggesting a premium, not a discount, compared to industry peers. 3.  **Operational Performance Not Translating to Expected Profitability:** Despite achieving massive production growth (up 540% quarter-over-quarter) and record Adjusted EBITDA (up 600% quarter-over-quarter), the second quarter 2025 revenue (\$68.1M) and Earnings Per Share (\$0.18) significantly missed analyst consensus estimates (\$134.9M and \$1.21, respectively), undermining the idea that operational success automatically closes the "market disconnect."

5.  **Hedge Strategy Caps Upside:** The "aggressive financial contracts (hedges)" designed to lock in stable cash flow for years will also severely cap the potential for "significant stock appreciation" if oil prices rise substantially. With approximately 85% of current production hedged through 2028 at a price deck of around \$60-\$64 per barrel, the company is insulated from a crash but cannot fully capitalize on a potential oil price super-cycle.
6.  **Acquisition and Execution Risk:** The growth model relies on a successful "roll-up" strategy, but the recent acquisitions, including the $\$600$ million Bayswater deal, have been financed with significant debt and stock offerings, and one analyst report noted that "recent acquisition production disappointed," increasing the risk of the aggressive development plan facing future delays or underperformance.
