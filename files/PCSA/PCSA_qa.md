## Business Summary

Processa Pharmaceuticals is a research and development company focused on creating improved versions of existing cancer treatments and drugs for rare diseases. They operate in the pre-commercial clinical stage, meaning they do not currently sell products or generate significant product revenue. Instead of inventing entirely new molecules, their business model centers on optimizing drugs already known to science to make them safer, more effective, or easier to administer. Their customers are future patients suffering from these specific diseases and the doctors who prescribe the treatments, primarily across North American and international healthcare markets. The company funds its extensive operations and clinical trials by frequently raising capital from investors, with success depending entirely on positive drug trial results and subsequent regulatory approval.

## Business Model Risk

The most significant near-term risk for Processa is the inherent failure rate of clinical trials. The majority of drugs tested in humans do not pass all required testing phases due to safety issues or lack of efficacy. If the company's current leading drug candidates fail Phase 2 or 3 trials, the value of the entire enterprise would be immediately and severely harmed. Historically, this risk is the main reason why small pharmaceutical companies fail. A major long-term risk involves regulatory hurdles; even if trials succeed, agencies like the FDA may not grant final market approval, which would prevent the company from earning revenue. Furthermore, competition poses a long-term threat: a larger pharmaceutical company could develop a superior, first-in-class treatment that would make Processa's optimized drug obsolete before it even hits the market. Finally, the company faces chronic financing risk, as it must continually secure new investor funds (which often dilutes existing shareholder value) to cover its high research costs.

## Bear Case Scenario

The following critique identifies holes and flaws in the bullish thesis for Processa Pharmaceuticals, Inc. (PCSA), formulating a corresponding bear case.

### **Bear Case Thesis: Flaws and Faults in the Bull Case**

The long thesis overestimates the immediate impact of non-dilutive funding, understates the severe financial risk, and relies on a high-risk, binary clinical trial outcome in a competitive market.

#### **1. Severe and Persistent Financial Risk (Dilution is Inevitable)**

*   **Critically Low Cash Position:** The company's cash and cash equivalents were only \$1.2 million as of December 31, 2024, and management has explicitly acknowledged the need for substantial additional funding to continue operations beyond mid-2025.
*   **Recurring Dilution:** The assumption that the historical pattern of stock dilution will halt is contradicted by the company's recent actions, which included a public offering in January 2025 and an ATM offering in July 2024 to raise capital. A net loss of \$11.9 million for the fiscal year 2024 suggests a significant cash burn rate that cannot be sustained by current liquid assets, making future equity financing (dilution) highly likely.

#### **2. Out-Licensing Value is Significantly Overstated**

*   **Near-Term Cash is Minimal:** The near-term catalyst of the gastroparesis drug (PCS12852) out-licensing to Intact Therapeutics is less impactful than presented. While the total potential deal value is up to \$452.5 million in milestones, Processa only received a small initial option exercise fee of \$2.5 million.
*   **60% Royalty-Sharing Requirement:** Crucially, Processa is obligated to share 60% of all cash payments from the deal (excluding the equity stake) with its original licensor. This dramatically reduces the actual net cash infusion, thus failing to provide the "necessary funds" to significantly de-risk the balance sheet or halt dilution.

#### **3. NGC-Cap is a High-Risk, Binary Catalyst**

*   **Mid-Stage Trial Binary Risk:** The long-term driver hinges entirely on "significantly superior" interim data from the NGC-Cap Phase 2 trial, which is a high-risk, binary event typical of clinical-stage biotech. The initial Phase 2 data is expected in the second half of 2025. Any delay, unexpected side effect, or failure to meet the efficacy bar would likely lead to a catastrophic drop in share price.
*   **Unproven Blockbuster Potential:** The "blockbuster candidate" label is speculative. Capecitabine is a decades-old, off-patent chemotherapy. Successfully optimizing an old drug does not guarantee a massive partnership or acquisition unless the safety/efficacy profile is truly paradigm-shifting, especially in the highly competitive metastatic breast cancer market.

#### **4. PCS499 is Not a Near-Term "Reliable Revenue" Source**

*   **Early Development Stage:** The "secondary win" of PCS499 for rare kidney disease (FSGS) is still in the early stages of designing an adaptive Phase 2/3 study, with discussions with the FDA planned for later in 2025. This is years away from generating "reliable revenue" and does not represent a near-term stabilizing factor.
*   **Enrollment Challenges & Regulatory Path Risk:** The program has already faced difficulties, as a previous Phase 2 trial for PCS499 in Necrobiosis Lipoidica was terminated due to a lack of patient enrollment for this ultra-rare disease. This highlights the difficulty in executing trials for rare diseases, even with a potentially favorable regulatory path.

#### **5. Overreliance on Regulatory Expertise and Management Execution**

*   **Ongoing Litigation Risk:** The assumption of an effective management team and regulatory expertise is clouded by the company being involved in litigation regarding a license agreement. Litigation is a distraction that consumes management time and financial resources.
*   **Strategic Reprioritization Risk:** The company's decision to terminate a license agreement for another oncology asset, PCS3117, suggests that their portfolio selection and/or development strategy for some assets may have failed a cost-benefit analysis, raising questions about the efficiency of their "unique drug optimization strategy" in practice.
