## Business Summary

First Citizens BancShares, Inc. operates a large commercial bank primarily focused on the Southeastern United States, though it expanded its reach nationally following a major acquisition in 2023, gaining a significant presence in technology and commercial finance sectors. The core business model involves taking money from everyday customers and businesses (deposits) and using those funds to issue various loans, such as mortgages, business lines of credit, and commercial real estate loans, thereby earning profit from the difference between the interest paid out and the interest collected. They serve retail clients, small to mid-sized companies (B2B), and offer wealth management services.

---

## Business Model Risk

The business faces significant near-term risks related to integration and interest rates. Integrating the recently acquired, technologically focused Silicon Valley Bank operations is complex and failure to retain those clients or successfully merge systems could harm earnings. Furthermore, if interest rates rise quickly, the bank may have to pay much higher interest to keep customer deposits, squeezing the profit margin it earns on its loans. Long-term risks center on credit quality: if economic conditions worsen, borrowers, particularly those in volatile commercial real estate or specialized tech sectors, might default on their loans, leading to major losses. These threats, especially risks related to interest rate volatility and concentrated lending portfolios, were the primary factors that caused the regional banking stresses in 2023, demonstrating their potential to severely harm the bank's market position and earnings.

## Company History

This history lesson on First Citizens BancShares, Inc. (FCNCA) is designed to be simple and jargon-free, covering its long journey from a small local bank to a major national financial institution.

***

### **The History of First Citizens BancShares, Inc. (FCNCA)**

#### **Part 1: Inception and the Early Focus (1898-1929)**

1.  **Founding Reason:** First Citizens began in 1898 in Smithfield, North Carolina, under the name **The Bank of Smithfield**. It was founded with a modest initial capital of only $\$10,000$ to serve as the sole bank for the local community, which was heavily reliant on agriculture.
2.  **Original Goal:** The bank’s original goal was to help local farmers and neighbors rise above economic hardships by securely holding their deposits and providing sensible loans. This foundational, community-focused approach of stability and long-term thinking has defined the company for over a century.
3.  **The Rise of the Holding Family:** In 1918, Robert Powell "R.P." Holding joined the bank, marking the start of a multi-generational legacy that still leads the company today. The bank changed its name in 1929 to **First Citizens Bank and Trust Company** to reflect its growth beyond its original town.

#### **Part 2: Navigating Crises and The Growth Strategy (1930s-2010s)**

4.  **Headwind: The Great Depression:** During the Great Depression in the 1930s, when many banks failed, R.P. Holding (who became president in 1935) employed a strategy of careful, prudent management to keep the bank strong and stable. This success through a major crisis reinforced the bank’s culture of fiscal conservatism and stability.
5.  **Establishing the Parent Company:** In 1986, the business reorganized its structure into a bank holding company, **First Citizens BancShares, Inc.** (FCNCA), which is the publicly traded entity you see today. This structure allowed the company to diversify its financial services and prepare for broader growth.
6.  **Innovative Solution: Buying Distressed Banks:** From the 2008-2009 Great Recession onward, First Citizens used a key, "counter-cyclical" strategy of acquiring smaller, failed banks from the FDIC. This innovative approach allowed them to double their size, expand their physical footprint, and gain assets at a good value while their competitors were cautious.
7.  **Core Tailwinds:** A major tailwind for the company has been the uninterrupted, four-generation leadership of the Holding family, which consistently prioritizes long-term value over short-term financial gains. This family control has provided the bank with strategic stability that many other publicly traded banks lack.

#### **Part 3: The Transformational Leap to a National Player (2022-Present)**

8.  **Transformational Merger (2022):** In 2022, First Citizens completed a major merger with **CIT Group Inc.**, an industry leader in commercial lending and direct digital banking. This move instantly doubled First Citizens' size, pushing its assets past the $\$100$ billion mark and establishing it as a Top 20 U.S. bank.
9.  **Opportunistic Acquisition (2023):** The company made a high-profile move by acquiring the assets and loans of the failed **Silicon Valley Bridge Bank (SVB)** in 2023. This opportunistic acquisition was a masterstroke, giving First Citizens immediate, deep expertise in the high-growth technology, venture capital, and private equity sectors.
10. **Present Day Goal and Status:** First Citizens BancShares has surpassed $\$200$ billion in total assets, cementing its status as a major national institution and earning a spot on the Fortune 500 list in 2024. Their goal remains consistent: to use their size and stability to help all clients—from individuals to major tech firms—build lasting financial security.

## Moat Analysis

First Citizens BancShares’ moat is not built on a typical single product advantage, but rather on a unique and highly successful decades-long strategy of acquiring and integrating other banks, particularly failed or distressed ones, at favorable terms.

Here is an analysis of the company's competitive advantages and key differentiators.

### Core Moat: Specialization in Acquisitions

1.  **"Acquirer of Choice" for Distressed Banks:** First Citizens' primary competitive advantage is its unmatched experience and track record in executing complex, FDIC-assisted acquisitions, having completed more of these deals since 2009 than any other bank.
2.  **Gaining Scale and Assets at a Discount:** This expertise allows the company to buy large portfolios of loans and deposits at a significant discount, as seen with the 2023 acquisition of Silicon Valley Bridge Bank (SVB) and the 2021 acquisition of CIT Group. This process instantly and cheaply quadrupled its asset base from 2021 to 2023.
3.  **Financial Cushion (Loss-Share Agreements):** In major FDIC-assisted deals like SVB, the structure often includes a "loss share agreement" where the FDIC agrees to absorb a portion of the loan losses for a period, providing a financial safety net and reducing the risk associated with taking on distressed assets.

### Differentiation and Special Market Position

4.  **Entry into Specialized, High-Value Markets:** The SVB acquisition instantly gave First Citizens a powerful presence in the high-growth "innovation economy," including technology, private equity, and venture capital financing, which are higher-margin commercial banking segments.
5.  **Operating Leverage and High Profit Margins:** The bank's focus on these higher-margin commercial segments, combined with the efficiency gains from integrating large acquisitions, has led to a high profit margin that exceeds many of its regional banking peers.
6.  **Blending a National Specialized Bank with a Stable Regional/Community Base:** First Citizens maintains a dual identity, combining the specialized, national commercial capabilities gained from SVB and CIT with its legacy of a strong, stable, and diversified community and regional banking network.

### Customer Stickiness and Recurring Revenue

7.  **Sticky Commercial Banking Relationships:** The commercial and corporate clients, particularly those inherited from SVB in the tech and life sciences sectors, often have complex, recurring banking needs for services like credit lines, treasury management, and specialized wealth management, making these contracts highly sticky.
8.  **Inherent Switching Costs:** Like most banks, a natural level of customer stickiness exists because it is inconvenient for customers to change all their direct deposits, automated bill payments, and other tied-in financial services.
9.  **Focus on Client Retention:** The company places a significant emphasis on client retention, particularly after acquisitions, by ensuring a seamless transition and building loyalty through personalized, high-quality service and a broad product range.

### Displacement Risk and Continuous Advantage

10. **Difficult-to-Replicate Growth Engine:** While capital is essential in banking, a company with merely more capital cannot easily replicate the growth. The core skill is the unique ability to successfully bid for, integrate, and manage the risk of large, distressed, and complex bank acquisitions—an opportunity that arises infrequently and requires deep institutional experience and regulatory approval.
11. **Technology Investment is Necessary, Not the Moat:** The company is continuously investing in technology and risk management to improve its service offerings and compete with traditional banks and emerging fintech. This is less of a unique moat and more of a **cost of doing business** to maintain its competitive edge and client experience in the evolving industry.
12. **Specialized Network Effects (Limited):** The specialization in tech and venture capital clients from the SVB deal provides a form of network effect; being the preferred bank for a critical mass of interconnected companies in a specific industry makes it harder for others to penetrate the market.

## Bear Case Scenario

The long thesis for First Citizens BancShares, Inc. (FCNCA) focuses heavily on the opportunistic nature of the acquisitions and the potential for long-term growth in the "innovation economy." A bear case must focus on the *risks and execution challenges* embedded in this rapid transformation.

Here are the holes and faults in the long thesis and the resulting bear case:

---

### **Critique & Flaws in the Long Thesis**

#### **Flaws in Assumption 1: Successful and Smooth Integration & Client Retention**

1.  **Mounting and Protracted Integration Costs:** The assumption of a "smoothly completed" integration is challenged by ongoing, significant acquisition-related costs, including increased personnel expenses and an impairment charge for software/projects, which suggests operational friction and higher-than-expected expenses from merging the vastly different platforms of legacy FCNCA, CIT, and SVB.
2.  **Tech Sector Headwinds and Client Churn Risk:** While the bull case touts the high-profit tech/VC clients, First Citizens' own management has acknowledged "headwinds" in the innovation economy, with pressure on growth stemming from a slowdown in private equity and venture capital funding. This environment risks client migration to major money centers once the FDIC-induced stability period fully passes.
3.  **Deposit Base Instability:** The SVB client base is notoriously transactional and sensitive to interest rates, with the company experiencing some deposit decline in recent quarters due to clients shifting funds off-balance sheet. This volatility undermines the thesis of a guaranteed "stable, low-cost deposit base" compared to traditional regional peers.

#### **Flaws in Assumption 2: Economic Stability in Key Sectors (CRE & Tech) for Loan Quality**

4.  **Significant Commercial Real Estate (CRE) Exposure:** FCNCA has substantial exposure to CRE, with the General Office CRE portfolio alone accounting for a notable percentage of total loans. A downturn in the challenged office or general CRE market could lead to substantial losses that are not fully covered by the FDIC loss-share agreement on the acquired SVB assets.
5.  **Evidence of Elevated Credit Risk:** The loan book is exhibiting signs of increased credit deterioration, as shown by a rise in nonaccrual loans to nearly 1% of total loans (0.97% as of Q3 2025). Furthermore, a single, large $82 million charge-off in the Commercial Bank segment highlights the elevated, idiosyncratic risk within the acquired or expanded loan portfolios.
6.  **Diminishing Purchase Accounting Benefits:** The "discounted acquisition" profit is heavily dependent on purchase accounting accretion (PAA), which is a finite and declining source of income as time passes. This non-core revenue stream has begun to decrease, creating a headwind to the Net Interest Margin (NIM).

#### **Flaws in Assumption 3: Maintenance of Conservative, Stable Risk Management**

7.  **Increased Regulatory Scrutiny and Complexity:** The bank nearly doubled in size with the SVB acquisition, accelerating its ascent into a larger regulatory class. This rapid growth increases regulatory compliance costs, capital requirements, and overall operational complexity, which runs counter to the bank's historical identity as a conservative regional player.
8.  **Management Bandwidth and Acquisition Fatigue:** The thesis overlooks the potential strain on management and operational bandwidth from attempting to integrate three major, diverse banks (CIT, SVB, and now a recently announced BMO branch acquisition) in a short period. This aggressive pace increases the risk of operational errors, missed synergies, and integration mistakes.

---

### **Bear Case Thesis for First Citizens BancShares (FCNCA)**

The bull case dramatically overestimates the long-term value capture from the SVB deal while underestimating the execution risk and macroeconomic headwinds associated with the combined entity's new, highly specialized asset base.

The FCNCA bear case hinges on the argument that the immediate, one-time profits from the SVB acquisition discount are masking two critical, rising problems: **deteriorating credit quality** in the combined commercial portfolio and **structural pressure on profitability** from a high-cost, high-risk operational structure.

1.  The large proportion of **Commercial Real Estate (CRE) loans and elevated nonaccrual loans** expose the bank to significant potential credit losses as the commercial property cycle turns, eroding the initial purchase discount benefit.
2.  The ongoing, **increased integration and personnel costs** from merging three disparate banks (legacy FCNCA, CIT, SVB) will weigh on the efficiency ratio and adjusted earnings for years, preventing the bank from achieving the superior profit margins of its specialized peers.
3.  The core SVB deposit base—which is crucial for the "low-cost funding" narrative—is highly **rate-sensitive and volatile**, leading to deposit outflows and a squeeze on the Net Interest Margin (NIM) due to increased deposit competition.
4.  The necessary shift to a complex national banking model with a high-beta tech/VC focus dramatically **increases the bank's regulatory and operational risk profile**, contradicting its history of conservative, family-controlled management and potentially leading to a discount on its valuation multiple.
