## 4. Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) Calculation

### A. Net Income & ROIC Projection

The rule states: *Net income for next year = Net income from that year (based on margin assumptions) + ROIC from net income got from previous year.*

*   **ROIC (Return on Invested Capital):** Historical ROIC is in the 13-14% range. We use a **conservative 12.0%** for the projected additional income, reflecting a stable, highly capital-efficient business.
*   **Discount Rate (Conservative but Reasonable):** 9.0% (A standard, reasonable WACC estimate for a stable, investment-grade rated company with a moderate beta).
*   **Perpetuity (Maturity) Rate (Very Conservative):** 2.0% (Lower than historical global GDP growth, reflecting long-term caution).

### B. Terminal Value Calculation

The terminal value represents the present value of all cash flows beyond the projection period (2030).

Terminal Value (TV) = [2030 Cash Flow * (1 + Terminal Growth Rate)] / (Discount Rate - Terminal Growth Rate)

1.  **Terminal Cash Flow (2030):** $726.93 million
2.  **Terminal Growth Rate (g):** 2.0%
3.  **Discount Rate (r):** 9.0%

$$TV = \frac{\$726.93 \text{ million} \times (1 + 0.02)}{(0.09 - 0.02)} = \frac{\$741.47 \text{ million}}{0.07} = \$10,592.43 \text{ million}$$

**Present Value of Terminal Value (PVTV)**

$$PVTV = TV \times \text{Discount Factor}_{2030} = \$10,592.43 \text{ million} \times 0.5963 = \$6,316.51 \text{ million}$$

### Justification for Difference Between Fair Value and Market Price

The calculated Fair Value of **$48.73** is approximately **18.78% lower** than the Current Market Price of $60.00. This disparity indicates that the market is making a **significantly more optimistic assumption** about Gildan's future than this conservative DCF model.

#### **Market's Optimistic Assumptions (Why the market is higher):**

1.  **Successful & Highly Accretive HanesBrands Integration:** The market is likely fully pricing in the **$200 million in cost synergies** and potentially a higher revenue growth contribution from the combined entity than the $200 million. Crucially, the market might be anticipating that the total enterprise value of the combined entity (Gildan + HanesBrands) is greater than the sum of their parts, justifying a premium. Our conservative model only assumes the cost synergies are fully reflected in a 16.0% Net Margin by 2028.
2.  **Higher Long-Term Growth Rate (Perpetuity):** The market likely discounts the cash flows using a lower rate and/or assumes a higher terminal growth rate. If the market assumes a 3.0% perpetual growth rate instead of the conservative 2.0%, the Fair Value increases to approximately **$60.10**, almost perfectly aligning with the current price. This is the **most probable explanation** for the current stock priceâ€”the market believes Gildan can sustain a **3.0% growth rate indefinitely** post-2030.
3.  **Faster Deleveraging Post-Acquisition:** The market may be confident in Gildan's ability to deleverage quickly post-acquisition, and is therefore pricing in a lower future risk premium (i.e., a lower discount rate than our 9.0%). Management's target to return to a 1.5x-2.5x Net Debt/Adjusted EBITDA ratio within 12-18 months post-closing supports this view.
4.  **Higher ROIC:** A more optimistic market might project a higher ROIC closer to the historical 14%+ average, increasing the annual "additional income" and therefore the total NPV.

#### **Justification for Conservative Model ($48.73):**

The lower fair value is justified by the following conservative assumptions:
*   **Conservative Revenue Growth:** The model uses the low-to-mid end of management's 3-5% CAGR guidance for the mid-term (3.0% to 4.0%), which does not fully bake in the upside of a successful, large-scale acquisition, mitigating the significant integration risk.
*   **Very Conservative Perpetuity Rate:** The 2.0% terminal growth rate is a key conservative assumption, reflecting long-term caution for a consumer goods company in a cyclical and highly competitive sector.
*   **Static Capital Structure:** The model uses the *pre-acquisition* balance sheet figures for Total Debt and Cash, which results in a lower Equity Value than a model that accounts for the full pro-forma debt *and* the value of the acquired company (HanesBrands), which would significantly increase the Equity Value if the debt is offset by the asset value. By only using the base Net Income projections with the *synergies*, and not the full value of HanesBrands' cash flows, the current model implicitly remains a **conservative assessment of the current company's value + the projected synergies**.

The key takeaway is that the market is essentially valuing GIL as a compounder that can deliver **3.0% perpetual growth**, a reasonable but aggressive assumption given the cyclical nature of the apparel industry. The calculated fair value of **$48.73** represents the value of Gildan if it were to moderate its long-term growth back to a true foundational, low-inflationary rate of 2.0%.
