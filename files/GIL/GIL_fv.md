### Terminal Value Calculation (as of EOY 2030)

$$Terminal Value = \frac{FCFE_{2030} \times (1 + \text{Terminal Growth Rate})}{\text{Discount Rate} - \text{Terminal Growth Rate}}$$

$$Terminal Value = \frac{\$2,846.2 \times (1 + 0.02)}{0.09 - 0.02}$$

$$Terminal Value = \frac{\$2,903.1}{0.07} = \$41,472.9$$

### Net Present Value (NPV) of Terminal Value

$$NPV(\text{Terminal Value}) = \text{Terminal Value} \times \text{Discount Factor}_{2030}$$

$$NPV(\text{Terminal Value}) = \$41,472.9 \times 0.5963 = \$24,741.0$$

### Total Net Present Value (NPV)

$$Total NPV = \text{Sum of PV of Explicit Period} + \text{NPV of Terminal Value}$$

$$Total NPV = \$8,031.1 + \$24,741.0 = \$32,772.1 \text{ million}$$

## 4. Fair Value Calculation and Justification

### Fair Value per Share

$$Fair Value = \frac{\text{Total NPV} - \text{Total Debt} + \text{Total Cash}}{\text{Shares Outstanding}}$$

## 5. Justification and Market Comparison

### Fair Value: $208.43 per share

### Current Market Value: $\approx \$36.50$ per share

The calculated fair value of **$208.43** is drastically higher than the current market price of approximately **$36.50** per share.

### Justification of Discrepancy

The massive difference arises directly from the assumption required by the valuation rules, specifically the re-investment rule: *"net income for each year goes straight into cash for the next year. Then get a reasonable ROIC percentage based on the past to get additional income for the next year."*

**My Assumption vs. Market Implied Assumption:**

1.  **Reinvestment/ROIC Rule ($\text{Fair Value Driver}$):** My DCF assumes *Net Income* is a deployable cash flow (FCFE proxy) that is **not** immediately spent, but instead retained to generate a continuous **15.0%** ROIC in perpetuity, on a compounding basis. This creates an extremely high terminal value that dominates the total NPV.
    *   **Market Implication:** The market is clearly **not** assuming that 100% of Net Income is retained and reinvested at a 15.0% ROIC without an offsetting capital expenditure or working capital need. The market is valuing the company closer to a traditional Free Cash Flow (FCFF/FCFE) model, where a significant portion of Net Income is offset by capital expenditures (CapEx is guided at 3-4% of sales) and working capital changes required to support a combined \$8B+ revenue business.

2.  **Growth and Synergy ($\text{Fair Value Driver}$):** My model uses the low-end of management's 3.5% CAGR and the explicit \$200M in synergies.
    *   **Market Implication:** The market is likely discounting the certainty or speed of the HanesBrands acquisition (still expected to close), the realization of the full \$200M in synergies, and the overall growth rate of the *combined* entity. The political/competitive risks associated with doubling the company's size are substantial and are being factored in as a higher risk premium (implied higher discount rate) or a lower growth profile than management guides.

3.  **Terminal Value Weight ($\text{Fair Value Driver}$):** Due to the compounding ROIC rule, the Terminal Value accounts for over 75% of the total calculated value.

**Conclusion:**

The Fair Value of \$208.43 is a **mathematical consequence of the required compounding ROIC rule** applied to the Net Income proxy, combined with a relatively low 9.0% discount rate and a 2.0% terminal growth rate. In a real-world scenario, FCFE would be calculated after deducting CapEx and factoring in an increase in Working Capital, which would substantially reduce the annual FCFE and thus the Total NPV.

**To align with a realistic market valuation, one would have to assume a much lower terminal growth rate (closer to 0%) or a discount rate greater than 20% to offset the compounding effect of the ROIC rule, or simply use a traditional FCF calculation (which the prompt forbade). The current high fair value is a direct artifact of the required valuation methodology.**
