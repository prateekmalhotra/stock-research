## Business Summary

Science Applications International Corporation (SAIC) operates almost exclusively as a service provider to the U.S. Federal Government, particularly the Department of Defense (the military), various civilian agencies, and the intelligence community. This makes it a Business-to-Government (B2G) company, where governmental bodies are the primary customers. SAIC's core model is to bid on and win large, complex, multi-year contracts to provide high-tech solutions. This involves integrating new computer systems, providing cybersecurity expertise, offering specialized engineering support, and managing critical government technology operations. Their revenue stream is entirely dependent on securing and successfully executing these extensive contracts awarded by federal agencies.

---

## Business Model Risk

The most significant risk is SAIC's heavy dependence on the political climate and spending decisions of the U.S. government. As their primary customer, any delay in approving the federal budget, broad spending cuts (known historically as sequestration), or a full government shutdown immediately disrupts SAIC's cash flow, halts contract awards, and slows down existing work. This political uncertainty is a near-term concern because government budget stalemates have frequently occurred, causing financial disruptions across the sector. A secondary near-term risk is the high competition involved in contract bidding; losing a major contract renewal to a rival can sharply reduce earnings. The main long-term risk is a shift in government strategy where agencies decide to perform more IT services using internal staff rather than outsourcing to companies like SAIC, which would significantly shrink their available market and growth potential. Historically, budget cuts have proven that political decisions directly determine the company’s financial stability, making current political gridlock a persistent threat to earnings.

## Company History

This is a history lesson about Science Applications International Corporation (SAIC), a major technology integrator and services provider for the U.S. government.

### **Part 1: Inception and Founding (1969)**

1.  **The Founder and Vision:** Science Applications International Corporation (SAIC) was founded in 1969 by Dr. J. Robert “Bob” Beyster, a former physicist who worked at the Los Alamos National Laboratory and General Atomics.
2.  **Why the Business Was Founded:** Dr. Beyster was frustrated that big defense and energy companies were de-emphasizing government-funded scientific research. He wanted to create a company focused on pure science and high-end technical consulting to solve complex national security and energy problems for the U.S. military and government.
3.  **The Initial Business:** The original firm, initially called Science Applications Incorporated (SAI), started with a small contract to study the effects of underwater explosions for the Navy and focused on projects like nuclear power and weapons effects studies.
4.  **A Unique Culture:** Dr. Beyster pioneered an innovative employee-ownership model, where technical staff and engineers could buy company stock and were encouraged to be entrepreneurial and take on new projects.

### **Part 2: Growth and a Major Challenge (1970s–2013)**

1.  **Early Growth (The Tailwind):** A major tailwind for the company was the consistent and growing need for scientific and technical expertise across the U.S. government, especially for the Department of Defense (DoD) and intelligence agencies during the Cold War era and beyond.
2.  **Diversification:** Over the decades, SAIC grew rapidly, diversifying its services from just defense to areas like national security, energy, environmental services, and healthcare technology, reducing its dependence on any single government sector.
3.  **The Employee Stock Problem (A Headwind):** By the early 2000s, the employee-ownership model, while fueling growth, presented a major financial problem: as many early employees reached retirement, they needed to sell their shares, forcing the company to use large amounts of cash to repurchase stock without an open public market.
4.  **Going Public:** To solve this liquidity problem and fund future growth, the company went public on the New York Stock Exchange in 2006, transitioning from a private, employee-owned firm to a publicly traded corporation.
5.  **A Reputational Headwind:** A significant problem was the **CityTime scandal** in New York City, where a multi-year payroll system contract resulted in massive overbilling and fraud convictions for some individuals involved, leading SAIC to pay a settlement of over $500 million.

### **Part 3: The Transformative Split (2013)**

1.  **The Ultimate Problem (Headwind):** The most critical challenge was the Federal Acquisition Regulation (FAR) rules on **Organizational Conflicts of Interest (OCI)**. These rules prevented a company from advising the government on a new system (consulting) and then competing to build that exact system (technology development).
2.  **The Innovative Solution (The Split):** In 2013, SAIC executed a major, transformative split into two separate, publicly traded companies to resolve this conflict. The goal was to allow both new companies to bid on a wider range of government contracts without the OCI restrictions.
3.  **The Two New Companies:** The *original* company changed its name to **Leidos** and focused on large-scale technology development and complex engineering solutions. The *spin-off* company kept the name **Science Applications International Corporation (SAIC)** and focused on government services, information technology (IT) support, and direct technical advice to government organizations.
4.  **The Current Identity:** The SAIC we know today is this spin-off company, which retained the historical name but became entirely focused on acting as a **technology integrator** for the government.

### **Part 4: SAIC Today (2013–Present)**

1.  **The Current Mission and Goal:** SAIC's current goal is to act as the essential technology backbone for the U.S. government, solving complex national needs by delivering secure, high-end digital solutions and IT services.
2.  **Focus on Digital Transformation (Tailwind):** A major tailwind is the government's pressing need to modernize its old, complex IT systems, which is driving enormous demand for services like cloud computing, artificial intelligence (AI), and cybersecurity.
3.  **Innovative Solutions for Growth:** To capitalize on this, SAIC created an **"Innovation Factory"**—a dedicated structure that rapidly develops and deploys enterprise-class solutions in key areas like AI, cloud platforms, and cyber, which are then integrated into customer projects.
4.  **Strategic Acquisitions:** SAIC has used strategic acquisitions, like Engility Holdings and the federal division of Unisys, to quickly gain scale and expertise in high-demand areas like engineering, logistics, and IT modernization for government clients.
5.  **Ongoing Headwinds:** The company continues to deal with headwinds like intense competition for government contracts, delays in new contract awards, and the uncertainty of federal budget cycles, which can lead to volatility in funding and contract volume.
6.  **The Business Today:** Today, SAIC is a Fortune 500 company, with nearly all its revenue (around 98%) coming from long-term contracts with the U.S. government, including the Department of Defense, the intelligence community, and various federal civilian agencies.

## Moat Analysis

The moat of Science Applications International Corporation (SAIC) is built less on proprietary products and more on a formidable combination of people, specialized access, and deep institutional knowledge within the highly regulated U.S. government market.

### **Analysis of SAIC's Moat and Differentiation**

1.  **Cleared Personnel as a Core Asset:** SAIC's most significant barrier to entry is its massive, pre-vetted, highly-skilled workforce that holds the necessary security clearances (e.g., Secret, Top Secret). A large portion of their employees are cleared for classified government projects.
2.  **Institutional Knowledge and Incumbency Advantage:** By serving a small group of critical customers, primarily the Department of Defense (DoD) and the Intelligence Community, SAIC develops deep, project-specific knowledge. This "incumbency advantage" makes them the low-risk choice for contract re-competes, as a new contractor would face high switching costs to learn the complex, mission-critical systems.
3.  **Differentiation by Integration, Not Invention:** SAIC does not typically invest heavily in internal Research & Development (R&D) to create proprietary products like a defense manufacturer would. Instead, their competitive edge comes from being a premier **technology integrator**, skilled at taking the best emerging commercial technologies (AI, cloud, cybersecurity) and securely adapting them to the government's highly complex, mission-critical systems.

### **Special Market Position, Pricing Power, and Growth**

4.  **Special Position in a Concentrated Market:** SAIC is one of the largest pure-play technology and service providers to the U.S. government, providing the necessary scale to compete for the largest, most complex, and multi-year contracts, which often involve integrating services across the entire life cycle of a program.
5.  **Limited Pricing Power but High Leverage in Specialization:** The federal IT market is highly competitive and contracts are subject to strict government-defined pricing (like IDIQ contracts). However, SAIC gains leverage and higher margins by focusing on specialized, high-demand areas like high-end analytics, cybersecurity, and digital modernization.
6.  **Reason for Growth: Strategic Scale and Acquisition:** SAIC grew into its current size largely through strategic acquisitions of companies like Engility and Unisys Federal, which significantly expanded its capacity, talent pool, and access to new customer segments within the government and intelligence sectors. This was critical for gaining the scale needed to win larger, classified contracts.

### **Contract Stickiness and Displacement Risk**

7.  **Sticky and Recurring Contracts:** The vast majority of SAIC's revenue is derived from **long-term government contracts**, making the revenue base highly stable and recurring, not one-time. The company operates with a large, durable contract backlog, which provides high revenue visibility for years into the future.
8.  **High Barrier to Displacement:** SAIC is not easily displaced by a company with merely "more capital." A new, highly capitalized entrant cannot simply buy the decades of institutional trust, the security clearances of thousands of personnel, or the deep, complex understanding of a military customer's specific operational needs in a short timeframe.
9.  **Network Effects are Incumbency Effects:** While not a typical software-style network effect, the company benefits from a form of **"incumbency network effect"**. Winning one major contract (e.g., in a specific branch of the military) leads to deep customer relationships, which in turn leads to a higher probability of winning follow-on contracts and task orders for related work.
10. **Focus on Innovation Integration:** To maintain its edge, SAIC must constantly innovate, but its focus is on **integrating** the latest commercial innovations (like AI or cloud services) into its solutions rather than on capital-intensive, internal R&D. The competitive advantage lies in being a trusted conduit that can rapidly deliver and secure emerging technology for the government.

## Management Incentive

Based on an analysis of Science Applications International Corporation's (SAIC) executive compensation and management incentive structure, primarily from its recent proxy statements (DEF 14A filings), the following points can be concluded:

### **Insider Ownership and Shareholder Alignment**

1.  **High Share Ownership Requirements:** SAIC maintains an Executive Share Ownership Program with high requirements for executive officers, promoting long-term alignment with shareholder interests.
2.  **Significant CEO Holding:** The CEO is required to own company stock valued at a multiple of their base salary (e.g., five times in one recent filing), and has demonstrated high compliance, with one report noting the CEO holding stock at approximately 16 times the salary.
3.  **NEO Shareholding:** Other Named Executive Officers (NEOs) are also subject to substantial ownership requirements (e.g., three to four times base salary), reinforcing a shared incentive for stock price appreciation.

### **Compensation Structure**

4.  **Performance-Based Pay Focus:** The executive compensation program is heavily weighted towards a "pay-for-performance" philosophy, with a large majority of NEO compensation being performance-based (on average, 88% in one reporting period).
5.  **Long-Term Focus:** A significant portion of performance-based pay is tied to long-term performance, often as much as 75% of the variable compensation, encouraging sustainable growth rather than short-term gains.
6.  **Use of Performance Stock Units (PSUs):** A substantial component of long-term incentives is granted as PSUs, which only vest based on achieving cumulative financial goals and market-based metrics over a multi-year period (e.g., a three-year cycle).
7.  **Multi-Dimensional Performance Metrics:** Incentive compensation (both short-term and long-term) is tied to a mix of financial metrics, including Adjusted EBITDA, Adjusted Operating Cash Flow, Revenue, and Relative Total Shareholder Return (rTSR) against a peer group.

### **Clawbacks and Shareholder Approval**

8.  **Clawback Policy:** The company maintains a clawback policy, which allows for the recoupment or repayment of equity-based awards under certain circumstances, acting as a governance safeguard against misconduct or excessive risk-taking.
9.  **Strong Shareholder Support:** The compensation plan has historically received strong approval from shareholders through the non-binding "Say on Pay" vote, indicating satisfaction with the program's structure and alignment.

### **Conclusion on Incentives**

10. **Incentives Favor Shareholders:** The management and directors of SAIC appear to have **sufficient incentive to act in the interest of the shareholders**. The combination of demanding stock ownership guidelines, a compensation structure that is heavily performance-based and long-term focused (using metrics like rTSR), and a clawback policy all work to align executive financial success with the creation of long-term shareholder value.
11. **Discouragement of Short-Term Focus:** The multi-year vesting periods for PSUs and the use of relative Total Shareholder Return as a key metric directly discourage actions aimed at "lining their own pockets" via short-term financial engineering or excessive risk.

## Long Thesis

SAIC is well-positioned for a rebound driven by internal efficiency and major, non-negotiable government spending on modernization. In the near term, aggressive cost-cutting and a renewed management focus on execution will boost operating margins and free cash flow, supporting dividends and share buybacks. Long term, SAIC acts as the essential, trusted partner for the government's shift to advanced technology (like Cloud, Cybersecurity, and AI). Its massive scale, decades of institutional trust, and specialized security clearances create high barriers for competitors. By focusing investment on high-margin digital services and successfully winning larger, more profitable modernization contracts, SAIC will achieve sustainable revenue growth and margin expansion.

---

## Long Thesis Assumptions

The most critical assumption is that the U.S. Federal Government maintains or increases its defense and technology spending, avoiding major budget cuts or prolonged government shutdowns that could disrupt contract funding. The bull case also assumes that SAIC's management successfully executes its new efficiency plan, hitting its margin targets (approaching 10%), and that its restructured business development team significantly improves the quality and win rate of bids for large, high-value AI and digital transformation contracts.

## Bear Case Scenario

The long thesis for Science Applications International Corporation (SAIC) is highly dependent on successful execution and a favorable, yet fiercely competitive, market. The primary faults and holes in the thesis revolve around the source of margin expansion, intense competitive pressure, and the precarious nature of achieving simultaneous top-line growth and margin expansion in the federal contracting space.

Here is a critique of the bull case:

### Critique: Holes and Faults in the Long Thesis

1.  **Margin Expansion is Driven by Cost-Cutting, Not Just Profitable Revenue Growth:** While SAIC has reaffirmed its Adjusted EBITDA margin targets (in the 9.3%-9.5% range for FY2026) and shown strong quarterly performance, this efficiency is occurring alongside pressure on the top line. For instance, the company *lowered* its FY2026 revenue guidance from prior projections, and quarterly revenues have dipped, suggesting that cost-cutting and a beneficial contract *mix shift* are fueling margin expansion, not necessarily sustainable revenue acceleration.

2.  **Intense Competition Erodes "High Barriers" and Market Share:** The claim that massive scale and trust create high barriers is countered by aggressive market competition, particularly in the shift to modern IT. Competitors like CACI and Leidos are actively unseating incumbents for large, high-value contracts. Most notably, CACI won the $5.7 billion Air Force EITaaS contract, which SAIC was the incumbent on, and SAIC chose not to bid on the Cloud One Next reseller portion of a $1.6 billion Air Force program, highlighting a selective (or unsuccessful) approach in major, all-encompassing digital transformation programs.

3.  **Dependence on External Technology Partners for "Digital Shift":** SAIC's pivot to high-margin digital services (Cloud, AI) is heavily reliant on commercial partners like Google, AWS, and Microsoft. While beneficial, this makes SAIC an *integrator* rather than a sole proprietor of the core technology, potentially capping its value-add and making it vulnerable to other integrators (like Accenture, Leidos, or IBM) who have similar partnerships and competing expertise.

4.  **Federal Budget Volatility Remains an Acute Risk to Revenue:** The underlying assumption of non-negotiable government spending is overly optimistic. Beyond the explicit risk of prolonged government shutdowns, broader government efficiency drives (like the Department of Government Efficiency, or DOGE, initiatives) can lead to cancellations of thousands of contracts labeled as "non-mission critical," putting persistent pressure on the civilian and labor-heavy portions of SAIC's portfolio.

5.  **Book-to-Bill Ratio Trend May Signal Future Revenue Pressure:** While quarterly bookings can be strong (e.g., 1.5 in Q2 FY2026), the trailing twelve-month (TTM) book-to-bill ratio has been at or below 1.0 in recent reports, which is the bare minimum needed to maintain the current revenue base and signals a potential deceleration in future revenue growth, contradicting the goal of "sustainable revenue growth."

***

### Bear Case Thesis

**SAIC is a mature, legacy integrator that faces a slow-growth environment where its margin expansion is primarily the result of cost-cutting and portfolio *optimization* rather than true revenue acceleration in the most competitive parts of the market.** The company's core business in traditional IT is stagnating or shrinking, forcing it to compete aggressively in the highly contested, lower-barrier-to-entry digital services market (Cloud/AI).

1.  **Top-Line Stagnation:** The market has peaked for SAIC's integration-heavy model, and new contracts, while highly digital, are not large enough or won frequently enough to offset the natural decay and loss of large incumbent contracts to competitors who are proving to be more successful at capturing next-generation, multi-billion-dollar IT modernization programs (e.g., CACI, Leidos, Accenture).
2.  **Margin Headwinds Follow Cost-Cutting:** The initial boost from "aggressive cost-cutting" will eventually plateau. Sustaining further margin expansion toward the 10% goal will become increasingly difficult without substantial, high-growth revenue, forcing the company to acquire or risk a new cycle of margin compression due to labor inflation and intense pricing pressure from smaller, more agile competitors.
3.  **Capital Return Over Investment:** A heavy focus on dividends and share buybacks (as the thesis highlights) in a low-growth environment may signal a lack of high-return investment opportunities. This conservative capital deployment suggests management prioritizes returning cash to shareholders over making necessary, large-scale, transformative investments to jump-start long-term organic revenue growth.
