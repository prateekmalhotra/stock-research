## **5. Conclusion and Justification**

**Fair Value Per Share:** **\$71.30**
**Current Market Price:** **\$45.02**

The calculated fair value of **\$71.30** per share is significantly higher than the current market price of **\$45.02**. This suggests the stock is **undervalued** by the market based on the conservative assumptions used in this DCF model.

### **Justification for the Valuation Difference**

The difference between the intrinsic value and the market price stems from the market making significantly different, more conservative assumptions about future cash flows, primarily regarding the **commodity price deck** and the **sustainability of growth**.

1.  **Commodity Price Conservatism (Market vs. Model):**
    *   The model uses a mid-cycle, conservative price deck (**\$75/bbl oil, \$3.50/mcf gas**) that is still supported by management's stated economics (50%+ ROR at \$50/bbl). The market, particularly for E&P companies, often embeds a lower long-term commodity price (e.g., \$60/bbl oil) in its valuation due to the cyclical and volatile nature of energy prices.
2.  **Discounting of Growth:**
    *   The model's growth assumptions (**3.5% for 2026, tapering to 1.5%**) are directly supported by management's **2-5% organic growth guidance** and projected cost efficiencies. The market may heavily discount this growth, assuming a quicker fade due to potential recession, future CapEx increases, or reserve depletion, which is a constant challenge for E&P companies.
3.  **Valuation of Midstream Assets (San Mateo):**
    *   Management has repeatedly stated their belief that the value of their midstream segment, San Mateo Midstream, is **"not fully realized in Matadorâ€™s current share price"** [cite: 8, Step 2]. The midstream business typically trades at a higher multiple due to its stable, fee-based cash flows. By using a single blended Net Income Margin (18.0%), this DCF model may not fully capture the higher, more stable value of the midstream segment, and the market is likely also failing to credit this value.
4.  **ROIC and Cash Generation:**
    *   The model gives credit to the company's high ROIC opportunity by projecting an **8.0% return on retained cash** being reinvested in high-return wells (>50% ROR). This compounding effect adds significant value in the outer years. The market, by focusing on current P/E or free cash flow, often fails to fully appreciate the value of an extensive, highly-economic inventory (2,000+ high-return locations) that can sustain returns on capital over a long period.

**Conclusion:** The market's assumptions are overly conservative, likely pricing in a substantially lower long-term commodity price and a higher risk premium on Matador's ability to sustain its planned organic growth and operational efficiencies, particularly overlooking the value of the integrated midstream business and high-return drilling inventory. The DCF model suggests the market is under-appreciating Matador's future profitability and capital efficiency.
